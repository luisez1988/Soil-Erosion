Effect of waves

Waves can have a significant effect on the scour depth around structures. This is because velocities are orbital which reverses flow direction periodically
\(KC\): Is the ratio of theoretical periodical particle displacement to a characteristic length.
\(KC=\cfrac{U_m T_w}{D}\)

\(U_m=\) Maximum value of undisturbed wave orbital velocity in the x direction at the bed.
\(T_w=\) Wave period
\(D=\) Characteristic length

The Keulegan-Carpenter number is related to the existence of horseshoe turbulent structures.

Alternatively, \(KC\) can be express as:

\(KC=\cfrac{2a}{D}\)

Where \(a=\) amplitude of particle motion

  • For small \(KC\) values, water stroke is small and wave have little influence.
  • For large \(KC\) values, water stroke is large and is similar than steady flow conditions.
From the previous figure, we can see that for \(KC<6\) horeshoe vortices are non-existent. Therefore, the scour depth is small.
  • Note that horeshoe vortices are formed in the back and front of the pile.
  • Horeshoe vortices are unsteady. They "live" for finite period of time.
  • Separation distance \(X_s\) is also a function of \(KC\)
  • With small \(KC\) values, flow separation is small.
  • For large \(KC\) values, separation distance is similar to steady state flow conditions.

Scour depth including wave effects

Scour depth is a function of the Keulegan-Carpenter number \(KC\). A larger \(KC\) value results in larger separation distance, larger stroke, and prolonged "life" of the horseshoe vortices.

For design purposes you can use:

\(\cfrac{S}{S_c}= 1- \exp [-0.03(KC-6)]\)

\(S_c=\) scour depth for current only

  • Use for \(KC<6\) only
  • Use for live-bed conditions.
