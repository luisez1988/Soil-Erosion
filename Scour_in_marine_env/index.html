Effect of waves

Waves can have a significant effect on the scour depth around structures. This is because velocities are orbital which reverses flow direction periodically
\(KC\): Is the ratio of theoretical periodical particle displacement to a characteristic length.
\(KC=\cfrac{U_m T_w}{D}\)

\(U_m=\) Maximum value of undisturbed wave orbital velocity in the x direction at the bed.
\(T_w=\) Wave period
\(D=\) Characteristic length

The Keulegan-Carpenter number is related to the existence of horseshoe turbulent structures.

Alternatively, \(KC\) can be express as:

\(KC=\cfrac{2a}{D}\)

Where \(a=\) amplitude of particle motion

  • For small \(KC\) values, water stroke is small and wave have little influence.
  • For large \(KC\) values, water stroke is large and is similar than steady flow conditions.
From the previous figure, we can see that for \(KC<6\) horeshoe vortices are non-existent. Therefore, the scour depth is small.
  • Note that horeshoe vortices are formed in the back and front of the pile.
  • Horeshoe vortices are unsteady. They "live" for finite period of time.
  • Separation distance \(X_s\) is also a function of \(KC\)
  • With small \(KC\) values, flow separation is small.
  • For large \(KC\) values, separation distance is similar to steady state flow conditions.

Scour depth including wave effects

Scour depth is a function of the Keulegan-Carpenter number \(KC\). A larger \(KC\) value results in larger separation distance, larger stroke, and prolonged "life" of the horseshoe vortices.

For design purposes you can use:

\(\cfrac{S}{S_c}= 1- \exp [-0.03(KC-6)]\)

\(S_c=\) scour depth for current only

  • Use for \(KC<6\) only
  • Use for live-bed conditions.

Effect of shape

The previous figures show that the shape of the pile has a significant effect on the scour depth.

For square piles oriented at 90\(^o\) with respect to the flow use the following to account for wave effects only:

\(\cfrac{S}{S_c}= 1- \exp [-0.015(KC-11)] \)

  • Use for live-bed only.
  • Use for \(KC>11\) only.

For square piles oriented at 45\(^o\) with respect to the flow use the following to account for wave effects only:

\(\cfrac{S}{S_c}= 1- \exp [-0.019(KC-3)] \)

  • Use for live-bed only.
  • Use for \(KC>3\) only.

Combined currents and waves

Combined effects of waves and current are accounted using a dimensionless velocity parameter.
\(U_{cw}\) is defined as:
\(U_{cw}=\cfrac{U_c}{U_c+U_w}\)

\(U_c=\) current velocity
\(U_w=\) wave velocity
Note: \(U_w\) is not the wave celerity

You can use the following expressions to account for combined effects of currents and waves:
\(\cfrac{S}{D}= \cfrac{S_c}{D}[1- \exp \{-A(KC-B)\}]\)

where:
\(A= 0.03+ 0.75 U_{cw}^2.6\)
\(B= 6 \exp(-4.7 U_{cw})\)
Are empirical fitting coefficients

Scour below pipelines

Scour in buried pipelines occurs because of pressure gradients upstream and downstream of the pipeline. This generates seepage underneath and subsequent liquefaction.

Onset of scour

We can use a modified Shields parameter to estimate the onset of scour in buried pipelines.
\(\Theta_m=\cfrac{U^2_m}{gD(1-n)(G_s-1)}\)

\(U_m=\) maximum undisturbed orbital velocity at the bed
\(D=\) characteristic length
\(n=\) porosity
\(G_s=\) specific gravity

Scour with waves alone

Both, scour depth and self burial are function of the Keulegan-Carpenter number \(KC\).

Use:

\(\cfrac{S}{D}=0.1 \sqrt{KC}\)

to get equilibrium scour depth for live-bed scour.

Combined current and waves

To include the combined effect of waves and current use the dimensionless velocity \(U_{cw}\)

Use:

\( \cfrac{S}{D}=\cfrac{S_c}{D} F\)

\(F=\) factor depending on \(U_{cw}\) and \(KC\)

Where:

\(F= \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} a\cfrac{5}{3}KC^a \exp (2.3b) &,\ 0 < U_{cw} \leq 0.7 \\ 1 &,\ U_{cw} >0.7 \\ \end{array} \right. \)

Coefficients \(a\) and \(b\) are found using:
For \(0 < U_{cw} \leq 0.4\)
\(a=0.557-0.912(U_{cw}-0.25)^2\)

\(b=-1.14+2.24(U_{cw}-0.25)^2\)
For \(0.4 < U_{cw} \leq 0.7\)
\(a=-2.14U_{cw}+1.46\)

\(b=3.3U_{cw}-2.5\)

Problems associated with scour around submerged pipes

  • Scour can make the pipeline to act as a bean
  • The additional reaction forces in the soil can produce bearing capacity failure
  • Soil removal will change the natural frequency of the pipeline.
  • Changes in natural frequency may produce resonance

Limitations of equations

Ship propeller scour

Ship propeller scour is complex process of sediment transport produced by the interaction between the propeller jet stream , the bed, sediments, and structures.
  • The jet stream can be obstructed in the presence of a quay.
  • Mooring and docking maneuvers require complex use of propellers.
  • Very complex to simulate.
  • Scour depth is function of several parameters including:
    1. Propeller shape and configuration
    2. Jet stream velocity
    3. Vessel draught
    4. Water depth
    5. Distance to quay
    6. Sediment size
  • Not many empirical equations available.
