Scour

Is the erosion that results because of the interaction between water, sediments, and structures/obstructions
Scour is a complex 3D Problem with no analytical solution
\(\alpha \) is the ratio of instantaneous bed shear stress \(\tau_t \) to the undisturbed bed shear stress \( \tau_\infty \) .

\(\alpha= \tau_t/ \tau_\infty \)

As result we can define the following conclusions:
  • When obstruction is added, \(\tau_t > \tau_\infty \implies \alpha>1\), thus scour occurs
  • With time, as more area is available for flow, \( \tau_t \approx \tau_\infty \implies \alpha \approx 1 \), thus scour depth reaches equilibrium

We can approximate the scour development with time using the following model:

\(S_t= S_{max} [1- \exp (t/T)]\)

\(S_{max} \): Equilibrium scour depth
\(T\): Parameter controlling rate.

Scour in bridges

Scour in bridges in the leading reason of bridge collapse in the U.S. accounting for around 80% of total cases.

Procedures to determine the scour in bridges are well documented. However, the majority of these methods are empirical
The Hydraulic Engineer Circular 18 (HEC-18) is the reference manual for bridge scour calculation

Scour in bridges can be caused by:
  • Long-term erosion also referred as channel stability.
  • Contraction or global scour
  • Local scour
Long term stability: River Allier, France. (Van Dijk et al. 2012)
Summer and Fredsoe (2002)

Calculation philosophy

  • The U.S. Congress recommended that the FHWA apply risk-based and data-drive approaches to calculate scour.
  • Prior to risk-based calculations all bridges would have been design for scour for a 100 yr return period flood \(Q_{100}\) and then checked with a 500 yr return period flow \(Q_{500}\)
  • Note that hydraulic return periods are smaller than scour return periods, because there is a reasonable high likelihood that hydraulic design flood will be exceeded during the service life of the bridge.

HEC-18 guidance

From the HEC-18 manual: "The interdisciplinary team needs to apply engineering judgement in comparing results obtained with scour computations and available hydrologic and hydraulic data ... at the site to achieve a reasonable and prudent design. "
Such data should include:
  • Performance of existing structures during past floods.
  • Effects of regulation and control of flood discharge.
  • Hydrologic characteristics and flood history of the stream and similar streams.
  • Whether the bridge has redundant structural elements.

Contraction scour

Contraction scour occurs due to the strangulation of the cross section due to the piers and abutments of the bridge protruding into the channel
According to the HEC-18 manual, contraction scour cross sections can be classified into four cases.
  1. Overbank flow forced into main channel.
  2. No overbank but section becomes narrower.
  3. Relief bridge in overbank with no sediment transport (i.e., clear water conditions).
  4. Relief bridge with secondary stream in overbank with bed transport (i.e., live bed conditions)

Calculation of contraction scour

Calculation of contraction scour is carried out differently for clear water and live bed conditions.
  • In the case of live bed conditions the fully developed scour depth reaches equilibrium (\(\alpha=1\)) when bed transport in the upstream section equals the bed transport in the contracted section.
  • For clear water conditions the sediment transport in the contracted section is near zero and equilibrium depth is reached when the bead shear stress equals the critical bed shear stress.

Live bed equation

A modified version of Laursen (1960) equation is recommended by HEC-18 to calculate the contraction scour depth in live bed conditions.
$$ \cfrac{y_2}{y_1}= \left(\cfrac{Q_2}{Q_1}\right)^{6/7} \left(\cfrac{W_1}{W_2} \right)^{K_1} $$

\(y_s=y_2-y_0\)
\(y_s= S_{max}\) from general model

\(y_0=\) Original water depth at contracted section
\(y_1=\) Water depth at upstream section
\(y_2=\) Water depth at contracted section
\(Q_1=\) Discharge at upstream section
\(Q_2=\) Discharge at contracted section
\(W_1=\) Width of upstream section
\(W_2=\) Width of contracted section
\(K_1=\) Coefficient that depends on the bed transport mode.

Notes on how to use the equation

  • This equations are only recommended for coarse sediments (sand and gravel).
  • For Sand channels where scour holes might fill again on the falling stage \(y_0\) can be approximated as \(y_1\)
  • \(Q_1\) is the flow in the upstream channel that transport sediments. It does not account for the portion of flow in the overbank under clear water conditions.
  • \(Q_2\) is the total flow in the contracted section. Thus, \(Q_2 \geq Q_1\)
  • \(W_1\) bottom width of the upstream channel transporting sediments. Do not consider the overbank width if it is in clear water conditions.

Notes on how to use the equation

  • \(W_2\) is the bottom width of the contracted channel minus the piers width.
  • For case 1c, contraction scour of the overbank is computed separately of main channel.

\(K_1\) is obtained from the table below

<0.5 0.59 Mostly contact bed material discharge
0.5 to 2.0 0.64 Some suspended bed material discharge
>2.0 0.69 Mostly suspended bed material discharge

Clear water equation

The equation is based in a development suggested by Laursen (1963). The equation is:
$$ y_2=\left( \cfrac{K_u Q^2}{D_m^{2/3}W^2}\right)^{3/7} $$

\(K_u\)=0.007 for English units,
or 0.025 for SI units
\(D_m= 1.25 d_{50}\)

\(Q=\) Discharge trough the bridge of on the overbank area bridge associated with width \(W\)
\(D_m=\) smallest non-transportable particle

\(W=\) bottom width of contracted section minus piers width

Notes on the use of the equation

  • You can use the top instead of \(W\) as long as you are consistent trough the calculations.
  • A reasonable lower limit of \(D_{50}=0.2\) mm is applied.
  • Using \(D_{50}<0.2 \) mm will overestimate clear water scour.

Local scour

Local scour is result of local vortices . These are turbulent congruent flow structures.

In piers obstruction downflow currents produce a curly vortex that remove material around the pier also known as horseshoe vortex.

Behind the pier it creates wake vortices capable of eroding material.

Factors affecting local scour

The magnitude and rate of local scour in piers and abutments is affected by:
  1. Flow velocity: \(\uparrow U \implies \uparrow S_{max}\)
  2. Width of the pier/abutment: \(\uparrow a \implies \uparrow S_{max}\)
  3. Flow depth: \(\uparrow y \implies \uparrow S_{max}\)
  4. Pier length does not have a big influence as long as the pier is aligned with the flow.
  5. Bed particle size does not have a prominent effect on scour depth, but it modifies the rate and time to reach scour equilibrium.
  6. Angle of attack has a significant influence.

Factors affecting local scour

  1. Shape of nose can have up to 20% influence in scour depth.
  2. Bed configuration of sand-bed channels affect scour. Eg. dunes, ripples, etc
  3. Floating debris can modify the geometry of the problem affecting the ultimate scour depth.

Local scour around piers

Flow around piers generates horseshoe and wake vortices. The flow field, however, can be affected by shape relative to the water depth.
Is a pier with width \(a\) small in comparison to the water depth \(y\). It fulfills the criterion \( y/a > 1.4 \)

Increasing the width of the pier compared to the water depth affects flow field

Pier scour equations

Various model studies have been used for a wide variety of pier configurations. As a result of the many laboratory studies, there are numerous pier scour equations.

In the figure \(y_s/a\) is the normalized scour depth.

A comparison of various predicting equations found that an equations proposed by Colorado State University (CSU) enveloped all data.
This means the equation is conservative.

HEC-18 equation (CSU)

The HEC-18 equation is:
\(\cfrac{y_s}{a}= 2 K_1 K_2 K_3 \left(\cfrac{y_1}{a}\right)^{0.35} F_{r1}^{0.43} \)

\(K_1=\) Factor for pier nose shape
\(K_2=\) Angle of attack factor
\(K_3=\) Correction factor for bed conditions
\(F_{r1}=\) Upstream section's Froude number
\(F_{r1}= U_1/\sqrt{gy_1}\)

  2. Use of \(K_2\) is limited to the pier length being fully subjected to the flow field. Use of \(K_2\) will over predict the scour depth when: a) a portion of the pier is shielded; or 2) an abutment or another structure redirects the flow parallel to the pier.
  3. \(K_3\) comes from evidence that maximum scour depth is 10% larger for in clear-water and the presence of dunes. In the unusual case of large dunes, the maximum scour depth is 20%-30% larger.

Pier scour at wide piers

Flume experiments on scour depths at wide piers in shallow flows and field observations indicate the CSU over estimates the scour depths. To correct this, use a correction factor \(K_w\).
For \(U/U_c< 1\) use:
\(K_w= 2.58 (y/a)^{0.34} F_{r1}^{0.65} \)

For \(U/U_c\geq 1\) use:
\(K_w= (y/a)^{0.13} F_{r1}^{0.25} \)

Note: This factor should be applied when \(y/a< 0.8\); \(a/d_{50}>50\); and \(F_{r1}< 1\)

Scour for complex foundations

Research to develop the HEC-18 equation was done with solid piers with little attention to (1) pile groups, (2) pile groups + caps, or (3) pile groups + cap + solid piers . In this case the HEC-18 manual recommends using the superposition of scour components. However, there is no evidence showing that principle exists.

Superposition principle steps

  1. Scour should be designed for design flood or smaller discharge if this causes larger scour depths.
  2. Analyze the configuration of the foundation and determine the components of the pier that are exposed to the flow.
  3. Determine the scour depths for each components and add then.
  4. Plot the scour depths and analyze the results using an interdisciplinary group.
  5. Conduct a physical model study to increase bridge safety or reduce costs.

Pier stem scour

The pier stem scour is calculated based on a modified version of the HEC-18 equation:
\(\cfrac{y_{spier}}{y_1}= K_{hpier}\left[2 K_1 K_2K_3 \left(\cfrac{a_{pier}}{y_1}\right)^{0.65} F_{r1}^{0.43} \right]\)

\(K_{hpier}=\) correction factor to account the height of pier stem above the bed.
Note: The term in brackets in the equation is the same for the basic HEC-18 equation.

Pile cap scour

There are two cases for pile cap scour:
  1. The bottom of the cap is above the bed by design or by scour.
  2. The bottom if the cap is on or below the bed.

Case 1

The strategy is to determine an equivalent pier width and calculate the scour depth using a modified HEC-18 equation

Case 2

The strategy is to treat the pile ap or exposed footing like a short pier in a shallow steam of depth equal to the height to the top of the footing above the bed.

Case 1: Pile cap scour

The modified equation is:
\(\cfrac{y_{spc}}{y_2}= 2 K_1 K_2 K_3 K_w \left(\cfrac{a^*_{pc}}{y_2}\right)^{0.65} F_{r2}^{0.43} \)

\(a^*_{pc}=\) Modified pier width. Use fig. 7.7 from HEC-18 manual.
\(T=\) Thickness of the pile cap exposed to the flow.
\(h_2=h_0+ 0.5 y_{spier}\)
\(y_2= y_1+ 0.5 y_{spier}\) Adjusted fow depth
\(U_2=U_1y_1/y_2\) Adjusted flow velocity

Case 2: Pile cap scour

The modified equation is:
\(\cfrac{y_{spc}}{y_f}= 2 K_1 K_2 K_3 K_w \left(\cfrac{a_{pc}}{y_f}\right)^{0.65} F_{rf}^{0.43} \)

Subscript \((\cdot)_f\) refers to the flow zone below the top of the width.
\(y_f=\) considers degradation, contraction, and pier stem scour.

calculate the velocity using a logarithmic velocity profile:
\(\cfrac{U_f}{U_2} = \cfrac{\ln(10.93 y_f/k_s + 1)}{\ln(10.93 y_2/k_s +1)} \)

\(k_s\) is a roughness term that should be equal to \(d_{84}\) for sands and \(3.5 d_{84}\) for gravel

Note: Because for case two, the assumption is that the pier bottom will remain buried , we do not need to calculate the scour in the pier groups. therefore:

\(y_s=y_{spier} + y_{spc}\)

Pile group scour

The scour depth for pile groups is calculated as a function of the spacing , number of pile rows , and pile length exposed into the flow.

The strategy consists of the following steps:

  1. Project the width of of the piles onto a plane normal to the flow direction.
  2. Determine the width of an equivalent pier that would produce same scour depth as if pile group penetrates water surface.
  3. Adjust flow depth, velocity, and exposed height of the pile group.
  4. Determine group height based on exposed heigh of pile group.
  5. Compute scour using modified version of HEC-18.

Project width of piles

Piles aligned with flow

In this case the projected width \(a_{proj}\) is the width of the collapsed group

Skewed piles

The projected width \(a_{proj}\) is calculated as the sum of the non-overlapping projected widths times a spacing factor \(K_{sp}\).

Equivalent width of pile group

The equivalent pile group is then calculated using:

\(a^*_{pg}= a_{proj} K_{sp} K_m\)

\(K_m=\) number of rows factor

The modified scour equation is:
\(\cfrac{y_{spg}}{y_3}= K_{hpg} \left[2 K_1 K_3 \left(\cfrac{a^*_{pg}}{y_3} \right)^{0.65} F_{r3}^{0.43} \right] \)

\(y_3= y_1+ 0.5 (y_{spier}+y_{spc}) \)
\(U_3= U_1 y_1/y_3\)
\(K_{hpg}=\) factor of pile group heigh given in Fig. 7.13 from HEC-18
\(h_3= h_0+ 0.5(y_{spier}+y_{spc})\) height of pile group above the lowered stream bed after pier and pile cap scour.

Thus:
\(y_s=y_{spier}+y_{spc}+y_{spg}\)

Local scour in abutments

Scour at abutments occurs when the abutment and roadway obstruct the flow. Several causes have been identified

Causes:

  • Overtopping of abutment.
  • Stream widening.
  • Contraction scour.
  • Local scour

Flow around an abutment

The flow around an abutment generate rollers along the main channel slope. After the abutment, the flow show a high turbulent zone where wake vortices are observed.
Note: Bridge abutment locations in levees can weaken the stability of the levee due to erosion, which can trigger a levee failure, and subsequent flooding.

These structures are somehow similar to those observed in piers.

Factors influencing local scour in abutments

  1. Abutment shape
  2. Discharge in the main channel and the abutment.
  3. Discharge intercepted by the abutment and returned to the main channel.
  4. Sediment characteristics.
  5. Cross-sectional shape of the main channel and the abutment.
  6. Overbank vegetation or trees.

Effective abutment length \(L'\)

Typically the length the abutment (embankment) projected normal to flow is used as an independent variable. However, the appropriate length to be used in the length of embankment blocking "live" flow. This is referred as the effective length.

Froehlich's scour equation

Froehlich analyzed 170 live-bed scour in flume experiments and obtained the following equation by regression:
\(\cfrac{y_s}{y_a}=2.27 K_1 K_2 \left(\cfrac{L'}{y_a}\right)^{0.43} F_r^{0.61}+1\)

\(y_a=\) flow depth in the flood plain
\(L'=\) Effective length of active flow obstructed by embankment
\(F_{re}=\) Abutment's upstream Froude number
\(K_1=\) shape factor (Table 8.1)
\(K_2=\) skew angle factor

Calculate \(K_2\) using the following equation:
\(K_2= (\theta / 90)^{0.13}\)

\(\theta< 90^o\) if embankment points downstream
\(\theta> 90^o\) if embankment points downstream

Calculate \(F_{re}\) using the following equation:
\(F_{re}= U_e/\sqrt{g y_a}\)

\(U_e=Q_e/A_e\) and \(Q_e=\) flow obstructed by te embankment.
\(y_a= A_e/L\) average depth of fow on the floodplain

Note: Froehlich's equation is not consistent with the fact that when \(L' \longrightarrow 0 \implies y_s \longrightarrow 0\).
The "\(+1\)" in the equation was added so that it envelops 98% of data

The HIRE equation

An equation based on field data at the end of spurs in the Mississippi River (obtained by the USACE) is proposed to estimate scour in abutments:
\(\cfrac{y_s}{y_1}= 4 F_r^{0.33} \cfrac{K_1 K_2}{0.55}\)

\(K_1=\) shape factor (Table 8.1)
\(K_2=\) skew angle factor (Same as for Froehlich's equation)
\(F_r=\) Froude number based on the velocity and depth adjacent to and upstream of the abutment
Note: Use this equation if projected \(L\) to \(y_1\) is greater than 25

NCHRP 24-20 Approach

The National Cooperative Highway Research Program (NCHRP) developed abutment scour equations considering various variables including: i) abutment type, ii) locations, and iii) sediment transport conditions.
These equations use contraction scour as a base, and apply a correction factor to account for large-scale turbulence in the vicinity of the abutment.
There are three types of abutments considered:
  1. Scour near the main channel
  2. Scour for set back abutments
  3. Embankment breaches, and abutment acts as pier
If the length of the embankment is larger than 75% of the floodplain width, the abutment will be scoured as in case a under live-bed conditions.

If the projected length of the embankment is less than 75% of the floodplain width, scour condition b occurs and contraction scour should be performed for clear-water conditions.

The NCHRP 24-20 equations are:
\(y_{max}= \alpha y_c\)

\(y_c=\) Flow depth including contraction scour
\(\alpha=\) correction factor for live-bed or clear-water conditions
\(y_s= y_{max}-y_0\)

Live-bed factor

Clear water factor

Calculate the contraction scour using for live-bed using:
\(y_c=y_1 \left(\cfrac{q_{2c}}{q_1} \right)^{6/7}\)
And for clear-water use:
\(y_c=\left(\cfrac{q_{2f}}{K_{u1} d_{50}^{1/3}}\right)^{6/7}\) or \(y_c= \left(\cfrac{\gamma_w}{\tau_c} \right) \left(\cfrac{n q_{2f}}{K_{u2} }\right)^{6/7}\)

\(y_1=\) Upstream flow depth
\(q_1=\) Upstream unit discharge
\(q_{2c}=\) Unit discharge in the constricted opening accounting for non-uniform flow distribution

\(n=\) Manning coefficient in the floodplain
\(K_{u1}= \)11.17 for Eng, and 6.19 for SI
\(K_{u2}= \)1.486 for Eng, and 1.0 for SI
\(\tau_c=\) Critical shear stress for the floodplain material

Steps to use the NCHRP method

Is defined as the ratio of the set-back length \(S_L\) to the flow to the average flow depth in the channel.
\(SBR=S_L/\bar{y}\)

If \(SBR<5\), as in the figure, in both abutments compute:
\(U=Q/A\)
with \(A\) being the total area in the contracted section and \(Q\) the total discharge.
Then compute:
\(q=U y\)

If SBR>5 for and abutment, like in the figure, compute:
\(U=Q/A\)
with \(A\) being the partial area of the floodplain in the contracted section and \(Q\) the discharge of the floodplain only.
Then compute:
\(q=U y_a\)
where \(y_a\) is the average depth of flow in the floodplain.
If \(SBR>5\) in one abutment and \(SBR<5\) in the other, compute:
\(U=Q/A\)
a) for the abutment with \(SBR<5\)
\(A=\) area of main channel plus floodplain and \(Q\) is the discharge of main channel plus overbank discharge.
b) for the abutment with \(SBR>5\) same as in previous case
Then compute:
\(q=U y\)
In the main channel the unit discharge is calculated using the average flow depth in the main channel, and for the floodplain the unit discharge is calculated using the average flow depth in the floodplain.

Scour width calculation

For local scour, calculate the scour width using the following equation:
\(W=y_s (K+ \cot \phi_r)\)
Where:
\(y_s=\) scour depth
\(K=\) bottom width of the hole related to the depth
\(\phi_r=\) Repose friction angle of the soil

Final comments

  1. Erosion depends on particle size. Increasing particle size armors foundations against scour.
  2. Shape of pier affects turbulent structures. A streamlined foundation can reduce scour depth.
  3. Cohesion or cementation can also help reducing scour depth.
  4. Predictive equations are highly empirical and in most cases conservative. Judgement based on experience and physical modeling would help reducing uncertainty.
  5. Read appendix D in the HEC-18 manual for a comprehensive example.
